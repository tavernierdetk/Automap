# The Incident — design proposal (2026-07-18)

The prologue's fourth segment (`prologue.incident`) was
`missing_core_design`: one event that must produce three lessons, seed
three arcs, keep the Road trusted, and hide a truth the late game
recontextualizes. This is the Story Director + Lore Keeper's proposal —
concrete answers to all nine open questions, ready for the human's
judgment. **It is PROPOSED canon** (bible ruling R-006): the Entropy
prologue's incident was not fixed upstream, so this fills the gap; it
must reconcile with `Entropy RPG.md` and be ratified before it is
treated as owned.

## The event — the Weirgate Release

The capstone of a Gatewright education: newly-disciplined seniors (Caden,
Vec, Isaac, ~15–16) are brought to **observe a real Nudge**. The Road
predicts the ancient **Weirgate locks** — flood-gates above a market
town, packed for the harvest fair — will fail. The Gatewrights'
sanctioned Nudge: a **controlled release** that redirects the crowd and
drains the pressure, at the cost of a small **"acceptable" number** too
close to the gates to clear in time. The prediction names that number.
The Road is trusted; the students are there to learn that guidance
sometimes counts a cost.

The controlled release begins. **Caden cannot accept the written-off.**
He breaks ranks and wades in to pull people from the failing gate.
Moments later the Weirgate fails **catastrophically** — the flood takes
the town. Hundreds die where the prediction had promised a handful.

That is what the player SEES. Everything below the line "what actually
caused it" is withheld for the fragments.

## The nine questions, answered

1. **What physically happens?** A supervised Gatewright Nudge — a
   controlled release at the Weirgate locks — becomes a catastrophic
   flood during a crowded harvest fair.
2. **Who is harmed or lost?** The prediction's "acceptable" few (whom
   Caden reaches for), then the town — hundreds, far past the predicted
   cost. None of the three protagonists; a named Auregate figure
   (professor or peer, cast later) may be among the lost for weight.
3. **What does Caden attempt to save?** The people the Nudge wrote off
   as acceptable loss — specifically he pulls four from the gate.
4. **What punishment does Caden receive?** Public blame as the reckless
   student whose deviation caused the flood; **expelled, his discipline-
   mark struck** (a formal un-making), sent away in disgrace. The
   institution needs "a student erred; the Gatewrights were faultless"
   to keep the Road trusted — this IS the public explanation.
5. **What choice does Vec make?** Vec also wants everyone saved — but he
   **holds the line**, trusts the Gatewrights, and tries to pull Caden
   back. He watches one boy's autonomy turn a managed cost into a
   massacre. His consequential choice is obedience under pressure.
6. **What evidence does Isaac discover or conceal?** As a **Weaver**
   (the discipline that assists prediction-work) Isaac is near the
   Gatewright's **prediction-slate**. It already named the flood's dead
   — including names of people nowhere near the locks, one crossed out
   and resolved "elsewhere," as if written for a course of events that
   did not happen here. He says nothing and **pockets a copy**.
7. **Failed, successful, or off-Road Nudge?** **A successful Nudge**
   (the system worked as designed) that Caden's deviation turned to
   catastrophe — publicly. The withheld truth: the "acceptable losses"
   were chosen, the prediction may have been engineered, and the larger
   flood may have been written before Caden moved. Success and sabotage
   are the two readings; the fragments arbitrate.
8. **Separated afterward?** Yes, immediately — the scattering the
   prologue promises. Caden exiled (disgrace → **redemption**), Vec
   retained and quietly commended for holding the line (validation →
   **corruption**), Isaac graduates and begins his own inquiry
   (obsession → **antagonist**).
9. **Hidden in the first presentation?** The slate's content; whether
   the release/failure was already underway before Caden moved; the
   Gatewrights' foreknowledge; the true cause of the larger flood. The
   player-observed FACTS (Caden reached in, the gate failed, the blame,
   the scattering) stay true; the INTERPRETATION (Caden caused it) is
   what fragments overturn.

## Three lessons from one event

- **Caden → redemption.** "I saved four. Hundreds drowned. Was my mercy
  the poison?" Compassion is bound to catastrophic cost; trust in the
  institution that scapegoated him — and in his own heart — is broken.
  *(outputs: compassion_associated_with_catastrophic_cost;
  trust_in_self_or_institutions_damaged)*
- **Vec → corruption.** He wanted them saved too — and held the line,
  and was right to, and it still wasn't enough because one person chose
  otherwise. Lesson: individual mercy is a luxury that kills; only
  order — obedience to the better prediction — prevents ruin. Genuine
  goodwill becomes the justification for making people hold the line.
  *(outputs: order_associated_with_prevention;
  autonomy_associated_with_unacceptable_risk)*
- **Isaac → antagonist.** The slate KNEW. The Road does not guide; it
  reads, or is made to read, and whoever writes the prediction holds
  the real power. He must learn how. *(outputs:
  uncertainty_associated_with_power_or_truth; motivation to investigate
  or exploit the Road)*

No reading is initially irrational: Caden's mercy is human, Vec's faith
in the system is defensible, Isaac's suspicion is warranted.

## The pruning seed (incidental now, evidence later)

The slate detail Isaac keeps: a prediction that accounts for the dead
of a course of events **that did not happen here** — a name resolved
"elsewhere," a doubled tally, a mark only a Weaver would notice. First
presentation: a boy lingering at a slate, pocketing something — reads as
grief, or evidence to defend Caden. Late-game fragment: the slate was
written for another branch of a copied world; its "elsewhere" is the
pruning. **No mechanism words are spoken** — no branch, copy, prune,
prediction-of-you — only names, marks, and a wrongness.

## Fragment plan (fair, not false)

Replays add material information without contradicting a visible fact:
- **Fragment A (Isaac's eye):** what the slate showed — the "elsewhere"
  names, the doubled tally.
- **Fragment B (the gate):** framing withheld the first time reveals the
  locks were already failing before Caden entered the water — his mercy
  may not have been the cause.
- **Fragment C (the Gatewright's word):** the "acceptable number" was
  chosen, not fated; the larger flood served a prediction.
Together they recontextualize Caden's guilt and the institution's story
without erasing what the player saw.

## Revisable forks (the human's calls)

1. **Is Caden actually to blame?** Proposal: ambiguous, leaning "no"
   (the fragments exonerate him). A darker game keeps it genuinely his
   fault. This decides whether redemption is from guilt or from a lie.
2. **The lost named figure** — a beloved professor, a peer, or a
   stranger? Weight vs. economy of cast.
3. **The disaster's nature** — flood/locks is one vivid option; a
   crowd-crush, a fire, a structural collapse all fit "managed cost
   turned catastrophe." Flood chosen for clean staging and the
   "acceptable number too close to clear."
4. **Vec's active choice** — passive (holds the line) vs. active (physically
   restrains Caden, and always wonders if that's why four more lived or
   hundreds died). Active is stronger for corruption; crueler to author.
5. **Reconcile with `Entropy RPG.md`** — if the source fixes any of
   these, its version wins; this proposal yields.
